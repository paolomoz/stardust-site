05 · Effects & community

Effects & community

We are committed to developing Heathrow responsibly and sustainably, with a focus on the wellbeing of our communities and the environment. We are carefully considering the effects expansion may have on the environment and local people, and have planned measures to avoid, reduce or — where possible — improve the current environment.

7.0

Assessing the effects

Section 7 of the consultation document covers fourteen environmental and social topics. Each is assessed in the PEIR and summarised in the local-area documents.

The fourteen topics are: air quality and odour; community; health; noise; socio-economic and employment; transport network users; biodiversity; historic environment; land quality; landscape and visual impact; water environment; carbon; climate change; and waste. The EIA process identifies and incorporates environmental measures (often referred to as 'mitigation') to avoid, reduce or offset significant adverse effects, and looks for opportunities to enhance the environment through environmental design.

The topics that follow distil the four areas with the highest community concern — air quality and noise; community and health; carbon and climate — alongside the cross-cutting frameworks that bind them: Environmentally Managed Growth, the Community Fund, and our Property and Compensation policies.

7.1 / 7.4

Air quality and noise

Air quality and aircraft noise are the two environmental effects most directly experienced by communities around Heathrow. Both are addressed through hard limits and targeted mitigation.

Air quality: the influence of Heathrow on air quality falls sharply with distance from the airport. Outside the airport boundary, the main source of pollution is road traffic — most of which is not airport-related. During operation of an expanded Heathrow, air quality is expected to improve over time as cleaner road vehicles and increasingly modern aircraft meet tighter emission standards. Our surface access proposals — HULEZ, HVAC, public transport investment, and a phased reduction in colleague parking — are central to managing operational air quality.

Noise: our framework proposes a noise envelope that defines limits using contour areas or quota count systems. The envelope will control total noise emissions and will be regularly revised to become progressively tighter, incentivising airlines and the airport to develop quieter aircraft. Combined with the scheduled night flight ban, runway alternation and reflective alternation, the proposals are designed to deliver progressive improvement in Heathrow's noise performance compared to the 2013 baseline.

What is the noise envelope?

A formal limit on total aircraft noise emissions from the airport. It can be defined by reference to noise contour areas, by a quota count system, or both. As airlines and the airport adopt quieter aircraft, the envelope tightens — meaning communities benefit from the reduction.

7.2 / 7.3

Community and health

The construction and operation of the Project will affect the community in different ways — particularly in relation to homes, businesses, community facilities and services.

Construction will require the acquisition of 756 homes; their residents will be displaced — a significant negative effect. Some community facilities will need to be relocated. Some construction workers will need to move to the area temporarily, which may affect housing availability — particularly rented homes.

We have identified mitigation including: replacement of community facilities, public open space (including sports facilities and allotments) and recreational routes; compensation for home-owners in the Compulsory Purchase Zone and Wider Property Offer Zone; a Home Relocation Support Service; an independent Resident Advice Service; a Construction Workforce Accommodation Strategy to reduce pressure on private-sector housing; welfare provision and occupational health services within construction sites; and a Community Safety Management Plan. The economic and employment benefits of the Project are expected to have a long-term and major positive benefit for health and quality of life.

7.12 / 7.13

Carbon and climate

Carbon emissions from expansion must not have a material effect on the ability of Government to meet its carbon reduction targets, including carbon budgets.

The Environmentally Managed Growth framework commits Heathrow to: implementation of specific mitigation measures to ensure expansion does not have a material effect on Government's ability to meet carbon reduction targets; a mechanism for ongoing review and reporting of carbon emissions involving independent oversight; and taking appropriate additional action in the event the carbon objective is threatened.

Carbon emissions are considered in the context of action being taken at international level by the aviation industry and nationally by the UK Government. The framework treats carbon as a hard limit alongside surface access, air quality, and noise — all monitored annually with the right to grow contingent on staying within the limits.

8.

Growing within environmental limits

Expansion at Heathrow is not a choice between the economy and the environment — it must deliver for both.

Our framework — Environmentally Managed Growth — sets clear limits on surface access (traffic), air quality, aircraft noise and carbon emissions. These limits set the maximum level of effect we will be allowed to have as the airport grows, and will align with the policies in the Airports NPS. If acceptable to the Secretary of State for Transport, the framework and limits will be part of our approval to grow — and we will be legally bound to remain within them.

An Independent Scrutiny Panel will oversee future growth. Data will be published regularly online and a detailed Annual Monitoring Report will show how the airport is performing in relation to the limits. The Panel will have powers to enforce the limits, hold us to account, and require corrective action — including, if necessary, implementing its own mitigation strategy.

We will have to earn the right to grow

Environmental performance will become central to the day-to-day operation and growth of the airport. Heathrow's objective will be closely aligned with the objectives of affected communities — and growth contingent on staying within the published limits.

9.

Community fund

We are proposing a new Community Fund to help address the positive and negative effects of the Project and improve quality of life in the area around the airport.

The Airports NPS requires us to consult on five areas of the Fund: eligibility, size, duration, delivery, and source of funding. We have generated ideas on each and want to hear your feedback so you can help shape how the Fund works for your community.

In principle, we think the Fund could be used for: enhancements to quality of life (community activities, health, transport, environment); initiatives that enhance the benefits of expansion (Heathrow Academy, skills, jobs, apprenticeships); enhancements of re-provided facilities (additional play areas, school places); and additional or ongoing mitigation measures where it is not possible to identify or quantify the necessary measures at the time of submitting our DCO application. Funding sources we are considering include Heathrow funding of transport, environmental, community and health measures; a passenger levy similar to the noise levy concept recommended by the Airports Commission; and an airport charge paid by airlines.

10.

Property and compensation

We have developed five interim property compensation policies for owners or occupiers of land that may need to be acquired, or may be affected by expansion.

The five interim policies are: Residential Property Policy (covering the Compulsory Purchase Zone and Wider Property Offer Zone); Agricultural Land and Property Policy (covering owners of agricultural land, farmhouses and farm buildings); Commercial Property Policy (for larger commercial interests and small businesses); Property Hardship Scheme (for owners with a compelling need to sell before they would be eligible under one of the other policies); and Professional Fees Policy (covering fees incurred during the compulsory acquisition process).

The policies apply within three areas: the Compulsory Purchase Zone — the area we anticipate needing for the future expanded airport; the Wider Property Offer Zone — outside the Compulsory Purchase Zone but in close proximity to the proposed new boundary; and the Draft Development Consent Order Limits — the maximum extent of land we may need to take temporarily or permanently to support the airport. The interim Property Hardship Scheme is already in operation and will remain so until we obtain consent and construction has begun. All interim policies will be updated following this consultation.
