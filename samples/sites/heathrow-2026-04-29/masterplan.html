02 · Masterplan

The Masterplan

Heathrow's vision is to give passengers the best airport service in the world. The layout of an expanded Heathrow — and all the different parts or components within it — is crucial to achieving this vision. Our latest thinking on the layout of the future airport's physical infrastructure is called our Preferred Masterplan.

As well as providing excellent passenger experience, our Preferred Masterplan must also work for local communities, airlines, freight operators, commuters, our investors and other airport users — while being sustainable, efficient, and fulfilling our commitments to the environment.

An illustrative interpretation

The Preferred Masterplan is illustrative but represents an interpretation of how the end state could look when constructed within parameters. We expect to apply for some parts in detail and for some in outline, by reference to parameters.

3.1

Airfield

Since the first private aerodrome opened on the site in 1930, Heathrow's airfield has been continually adapted and improved, resulting in the strategically important hub airport which exists today.

Expansion will enable us to further improve our airfield, prioritising efficiency for airlines and passengers whilst balancing the needs of our local communities. Our entire Project has been designed around a scheme with the proposed new runway to the north west of the existing two runways, to minimise the effects of noise on local communities, including densely populated areas in London.

The new runway will be 3,500m long. The distance between the centreline of the proposed new runway and the centreline of the existing northern runway will be 1,035m. The position of this runway was considered to be the best performing option as it will minimise environmental effects and property loss.

What are taxiways?

Taxiways act as the 'arteries' of the airport, enabling aircraft to move between areas for parking, refuelling and boarding passengers safely and efficiently.

3.2

Terminals, satellites and aprons

Terminals are where passengers spend most of their time at an airport. Terminals 2 and 5 have both been named the world's best airport terminal in the last two years, and Heathrow has been voted the best airport in Western Europe.

Expansion is an opportunity to build on this and make our terminal capacity more efficient, support world-class passenger service, and build sustainability and great design into our plans.

New terminal capacity and apron space will be split between the expanded Terminal 5 Campus and the Central Terminal Area (CTA), comprising Terminals 2 and 3. Terminal 2 will be extended (T2A) with new satellites T2C and T2D, increasing capacity to 52 mppa. Terminal 3 is proposed to be demolished in the later stages of construction. Terminal 1 will be demolished. The Terminal 5 Campus will be reconfigured and expanded, increasing throughput from 33 mppa to 40 mppa, with a new terminal T5X built to the west.

What are terminals, satellites and aprons?

Terminals are buildings that provide all the facilities needed for passengers to board and disembark from aircraft. Some passengers board directly from terminals; others transfer to remote satellites. Aprons are the areas where aircraft park, refuel, load and unload — usually adjacent to terminals.

3.3

Roads and rail

Sustainable and resilient surface connections play a key role in ensuring our passengers and colleagues can reach the airport. With expansion, Heathrow's position as an integrated transport hub will grow.

A number of roads around the airport, including the M25 and local roads such as the A4 and A3044, will need to be diverted. The M25 will be diverted offline so the existing motorway remains open while the diversion is built. Junction 14 will have an enlarged roundabout to enable connection to the new A3044 north. A new Southern Road Tunnel will connect Beacon Road Junction roundabout to the Central Terminal Area, transforming access from the south.

Rail: the existing Colnbrook branch line is displaced by the new runway but will be realigned to ensure aviation fuel supply is maintained. Heathrow is also supporting and has safeguarded the on-airport development needed for both Western and Southern Rail to directly serve the airport.

3.4

Active travel

Walking and cycling are an important part of how an expanded Heathrow will be reached.

We are introducing bus priority and cycling measures across our surface access proposals. The diverted A3044 will include a cycleway and we are investigating the option of incorporating local connectivity. We are also investigating the suitability of the new Southern Road Tunnel as a route for cyclists accessing the CTA from the south.

3.5

Water environment

Three rivers — the Duke of Northumberland's River, the Longford River and the Wraysbury River — will be diverted as part of the project, alongside flood mitigation infrastructure.

We have designed our diversions to enhance water habitats where possible, and to support the wider water environment. Our proposals include a new river corridor running parallel to the M25 diversion, and improved flood storage to manage surface water at our scale of expansion.

3.6

Airport supporting development

Beyond runway and terminals, an expanded airport needs cargo facilities, hotels, offices, fuel facilities, maintenance bases and immigration removal centres.

These functions will be re-provided across the expanded estate. Some are relocated to make way for the new runway; others are introduced new to support the airport at scale. Our supporting-development proposals are illustrative and refined throughout the consultation process.

3.7

Parking

Passenger parking is being consolidated into two large multi-storey 'parkways' — Northern and Southern — near the strategic road network.

These parkways will be located near the M25 and the new road infrastructure to reduce journeys on airport and local roads. Drop-off and pick-up will be reorganised at terminals to support the new layout. Colleague parking is being separately designed alongside our colleague-travel proposals.

3.8

Displaced land uses and community facilities

Expansion requires us to relocate or replace some existing buildings and land uses, including some homes, businesses and community facilities.

We are working to identify suitable sites for re-provided community facilities. The Immigration Removal Centres at Harmondsworth and Colnbrook are among the facilities that need re-provision. We will continue to work with affected communities through detailed local engagement.

3.9

Landscape

The boundary of an expanded Heathrow will be carefully designed to improve the overall appearance of the airport and help it blend into the surrounding area.

Boundary design integrates noise mitigation, visual screening and ecological enhancement. We are investigating new green-belt-style boundary features that combine landscape mounds, planting and water features. The result is intended to soften the airport's footprint where it meets surrounding communities.

3.10

Utilities

Power, water, drainage and telecoms infrastructure must be expanded to support a 142 mppa airport.

Our proposals for utilities form part of the supporting development land use and are being co-ordinated with infrastructure providers. We expect significant local works to upgrade and re-route utility connections in step with the wider construction programme.

3.11

Phasing the expansion

Expansion is delivered across four phases, from the Northwest Runway opening in 2026 to the end-state Masterplan around 2050.

Phase 1 (Opening day, around 2026): the new runway opens. Phase 2 (around 2030): capacity reaches approximately 115 mppa. Phase 3 (around 2035): new terminal capacity comes online to support around 130 mppa. Phase 4 (around 2050): the end-state Masterplan is delivered, supporting approximately 142 mppa.

This phasing reflects the sequence in which infrastructure becomes operational. Some elements — like the runway — must complete before later phases can begin; others can be delivered in parallel.
