03 · Construction & operations

Construction & operations

We recognise that the construction and operation of an expanded airport will affect the day-to-day lives of local people. There will be some negative effects but also some positive ones. We take seriously our responsibility to those around us to minimise the potential negative effects of expansion during and after construction.

This pillar covers our construction programme, how we propose to operate the future three-runway airport, and the changes to airspace that will support it.

Read alongside

These topics are covered in three separate Heathrow Expansion publications: the Construction Proposals document, the Future Runway Operations document, and the standalone Airspace Change Proposal that follows the CAA's separate Airspace Change Process.

4.1

Indicative construction programme

Construction is delivered across the four phases of development set out in the Masterplan. The exact timings depend on the demands and needs of passengers and airlines.

Before main construction begins, and ahead of the decision on our DCO application, we expect to carry out a number of early works in 2020 and 2021 — including undergrounding overhead power lines and activities to reduce environmental effects.

Main construction is expected to start in late 2021 if our DCO application is granted, with roughly five years of work before the new runway opens around 2026. Construction activities will include removal of existing buildings and structures, the re-provision of some commercial and industrial properties, re-routing of gas, electric and water supplies, and significant earthworks. We intend to use as much suitable material arising from the earthworks as possible within the development site to reduce import or export of large quantities of material.

4.2

Construction and logistics management

Expansion will require a number of construction projects to take place at the same time, an extensive supply chain and a large labour force.

To spread the economic benefits of expansion across the UK, we plan to build some elements of the Project at off-site logistics hubs, distance from Heathrow. In March 2019 we announced our shortlist of 18 logistics hubs across the UK; four will be chosen in 2020, providing jobs and economic opportunities in their local areas.

Where we can't keep construction within land we require permanently, we will use temporary Construction Support Sites located along main access roads and as close as possible to main construction areas. Following feedback from Airport Expansion Consultation One in 2018 we have reduced the footprint and number of these sites to minimise effects on surrounding communities — for example splitting CS-1 into two working areas to protect the Horton Brook ecological corridor and reducing CS-10 to create a buffer with adjacent residential areas.

4.3

Managing the effects of construction

We will put in place a Code of Construction Practice (CoCP) — a set of measures and standards that we and our contractors must follow throughout the construction period.

The CoCP will help ensure a consistent approach to managing construction activities. We have developed and are consulting on a draft CoCP that we have discussed with local authorities and other key stakeholders. We will also implement a Construction Traffic Management Plan with controls on construction vehicle types, hours of site operation, and delivery routes for goods vehicles.

A Construction Workforce Travel Plan will encourage the use of sustainable travel modes by construction workers — helping to reduce effects on air quality, lower carbon emissions, and manage congestion. We will develop a Worker Code of Conduct setting out standards of behaviour expected of our workforce towards local communities. Working hours will vary by activity and across different construction sites; 24-hour working may be required for activities directly related to ensuring the new runway can be operational as soon as possible, with proposed reduced working hours, additional mitigation or activity restrictions in the CoCP where 24-hour working would cause unacceptable effects.

5.1

Runway alternation

Communities around Heathrow value relief from aircraft noise — and runway alternation is the mechanism by which we provide it.

Currently, during the day on westerly operations, we alternate the use of our two runways every half-day, switching at 3 pm. This gives residents living under each flight path predictable relief for half the day. We don't currently alternate on easterly operations because of the legacy of the now-defunct Cranford Agreement.

With three runways we will move to a four-day rotation through four mode allocations — segregated landings, segregated departures, and mixed mode rotated between the runways. The change between mode allocations happens twice a day (at 14:00 or 15:00, and at midnight). We are also introducing 'reflective alternation', so when the wind direction changes, communities continue to receive the respite they expected — something not possible today.

What is mixed mode?

A 'segregated' runway is used for either landings or departures only. A 'mixed mode' runway is used for both landings and departures at the same time but usually less intensively, because aircraft must wait for the runway to clear in either direction.

5.2

Ban on scheduled night flights

We are proposing a scheduled night flight ban from 23:00 to 05:30 — a formal ban replacing today's voluntary commitment.

Today there is no formal ban on night flights at Heathrow, but a voluntary commitment of no scheduled flights between 23:05 and 04:45. We have always agreed that a formal ban is necessary to control the noise effects of expansion. The Airports NPS sets out the Government's expectation of a 6.5-hour ban on scheduled night flights between 23:00 and 07:00; we have assessed different options to determine the right length and start/finish times.

We propose a 23:00 – 05:30 scheduled ban, using one runway for early morning arrivals from 05:30 with the other two runways becoming operational at 06:00. Recovery periods are restricted: late-running departures only until midnight, late-running arrivals only until 23:30 — except in exceptional circumstances. Combined with runway alternation, this gives every community at least 7 hours' respite between 22:00 and 07:00.

5.3

Directional preference

When winds are light enough that aircraft can take off in either direction, we currently default to a 'westerly preference'. We propose to change this to a 'managed preference'.

On long-term average, westerly operations account for about 70% of the year and easterly operations 30%. The remaining time the wind is light enough (below 5 knots / 6 mph) that we can choose direction.

Feedback from the Airspace and Future Operations Consultation showed support for measures that deliver respite from aircraft noise and a preference for Heathrow to intervene if planes have been operating in the same direction for a long time. A 'managed preference' would change the direction of arriving and departing aircraft based on a set of criteria — designed to limit overall noise effects, provide periods of relief, and ensure communities receive the respite they expect when combined with reflective runway alternation.

5.4

Early growth

If our DCO application is granted, we want to bring forward some benefits of expansion as soon as practical by making increased use of our existing runways before the new runway is completed.

Our application for development consent will seek to lift the current restriction of 480,000 air transport movements (ATMs) per year and grow the airport by at least another 260,000 ATMs as required by the Airports NPS. Testing has confirmed that we expect to introduce up to 25,000 additional ATMs before the new runway opens.

New navigation technology — including Independent Parallel Approaches and enhanced Time-Based Separation — allows more efficient use of our runways safely. To ensure early growth doesn't lead to unacceptable effects we propose strict commitments: Environmentally Managed Growth limits commence from the onset of early growth; the Noise Insulation Scheme is introduced from the same point; and the proposed 23:00–05:30 night flight ban applies from the outset of early growth.

What is the Cranford Agreement?

An historic agreement between the Government and the residents of Cranford that restricted the number of take-offs from the northern runway in an easterly direction over the village. It is no longer in place — its removal has unlocked the option of two-runway alternation on easterly operations.

11.

Airspace

The changes to our flight paths follow a separate approvals process to the physical expansion of the airport on the ground.

To obtain permission for new flight paths associated with expansion, we need to submit an Airspace Change Proposal to the Civil Aviation Authority — but we can only do that once we have DCO consent. Both processes are overseen by the Secretary of State for Transport.

We will not know the final airspace design until after our flight path options consultation in 2022, but we have considered the likely effects as part of our environmental assessment. We have already undertaken two consultations: 'design principles' in early 2018 and 'design envelopes' in early 2019. We received approximately 20,000 suggestions of 'local factors' to take into account when designing flight paths — these will be entered into our database alongside known sensitive locations and considered during the airspace design process.
